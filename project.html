Overview of the 'Race for Innovation'


Aims of Project

Innovation is vital for economic growth and competitiveness. As the world changes rapidly, it is essential for countries to stay ahead through innovation. This project aims to understand the recent trends in innovation and to identify the key drivers of innovation in successful countries.

Through this project, I hope to better understand the mechanisms behind strong innovation ecosystems.


Global Innovation; Trends during the last 10 years.


The 'Global Innovation Index' (GII) uses a wide range of indicators to measure a country's innovation performance. After downloading/cleaning the data in Python, I created a map visualization showing the global trends in the index.





The strongest innovation ecosystems are primarily in North America, East-Asia and Europe, with Switzerland ranked first. The notable risers were China (44.7 to 55.3), USA, South Korea and Germany, with some previously high-ranked countries seeing declines (e.g Canada, Scandinavia).

To examine the relationship between innovation spending and GII scores, I used a loop on Python to batch download World Bank API links on R&D expenditure data (as percentages of GDP).





Figure2 shows R&D expenditure followed GII scores, with those who saw GII rises showing R&D increases (China, USA, Korea), and those who saw GII falls seeing stagnant R&D (Finland, UK). For Belgium and Norway, R&D increases corresponded with declining GII scores, thus the relationship between innovation spending and its output requires further analysis.



Assessing the relationship between Innovative Spend and Output


To account for the lag between R&D expenditure and output, I used Python to construct a three-year average of R&D expenditure for each country. First, I merged this statistic with World Bank data on ‘High-Tech’ exports (in percentages, and current US$).

Second, I scraped data on scientific publications from ScimagoJR. To account for population size, I used World Bank population data to construct a 'publications-per-million' (PPM) statistic. Then, I regressed R&D expenditure on each innovative output, to create bubble and scatter charts. I found positive correlations for both regressions.







Figure3 shows how East-Asia outperforms the West, with China (e.g) having higher ‘High-Tech’ export percentages than the US, despite spending a lower percentage of GDP on R&D.

Figure4’s R2 statistic of 0.32 suggests 32% of the ‘PPM’ variation is explained by R&D expenditure. Notably, Switzerland has a high ‘PPM’ value and R&D statistic, which may explain its top GII rankings.

Both charts demonstrate that innovation spending has positive impacts on innovative outputs.



The Ease of Innovation


To understand the ease of innovation in different countries, I scraped data from the Economist's 'Business Environment Rankings'. This measure considers various factors influencing business decisions (e.g competition, taxes etc). Figure5 shows the changes in BER scores for the 12 highest ranking countries at 2018-end





Singapore and Switzerland’s Top2 ranks may explain their strong performances in ‘High-Tech’ exports (Figure3) and GII (Figure1), respectively.

I used OECD data to create a trellis chart displaying the trends in Venture Capital, (investment on new ‘high-growth-potential’ businesses), for 12 countries. I used Colab to calculate the 2-year percentage change in VC investments, to filter out ‘year-on-year’ fluctuations.





Figures5&6 together show Countries with strong ‘BER’ scores (Switzerland) or large rank movements (USA) showed large increases in VC. Countries with stagnating ‘BERs’ showed lower VC growth levels (Germany) and even VC declines (Sweden, Finland).


Overall, the data suggests that healthy business environments support VC activity and innovation.



Going Forward; Considering the 'Talent'


To assess the talent of different countries, I scraped data from Webometrics, to create a stacked bar chart showing the number of Top1000 universities in countries hosting at least one Top100 university.




Figure7 demonstrates the US’ domination in the quality and quantity of higher education institutions, with China, UK and Germany following. However, smaller countries such as Switzerland and Netherlands also perform well in the Top100 given their size.

Next, I scraped data on the ‘Global Entrepreneurship Index’ from the GEDI website, and regressed it on the percentage of individuals holding master's degrees (World Bank).





Figure8 shows a positive correlation between higher-education attainment and entrepreneurship performance, although the R2 statistic of 0.14 suggests a relatively weak relationship. Nevertheless, many countries with high GEI scores and proportions with master's degrees also performed well in the GII (e.g US, Switzerland, Netherlands).


Overall, these charts suggest the importance of investing in the youth to nurture innovation.

Data Cleaning and Challenges

Scraping data from Webometrics proved challenging, as I was blocked from web-scraping using ‘pandas’ (pd.read_html), so I used 'Beautiful Soup' to inspect the website's elements and scrape the table. Then, I used 'Tabulate' to convert the scraped data into table format and converted it to long-form using the '.melt' command.

Common issues during data cleaning included mismatched names between data sources (for Figures1,3,8) and missing values (‘NaN’) for certain countries, which primarily affected World Bank and OECD data. The “match” and “.replace” commands in Excel and Python were used to address these issues. To deal with missing values for the R&D statistic (Figures3&4), the years 2015, 2017, and 2019 were used for the 3-year average due to the low number of missing values and inclusion of important countries such as Switzerland, who only published R&D data every two years.
