The 'Race for Innovation'


Aims of Project

Innovation is vital for economic growth and competitiveness. In today's rapidly changing world, the race for innovation has never been more fierce. This project explores the recent trends, examining the relationships between key innovation indicators.

Through this analysis, I hope to better understand the drivers behind the strongest innovation ecosystems.


Global Innovation; Trends during the last 10 years.


The 'Global Innovation Index' uses various indicators to measure a country's innovation performance. After downloading then cleaning the data in Python, I created a choropleth map showing global trends in the index.





The strongest innovators are primarily in North America, East-Asia and Europe, with Switzerland ranked first. Notable risers were China (44.7 to 55.3), USA and Germany, while some previously high-ranked countries saw declines (e.g Canada, Scandinavia).

To study the relationship between innovation spending and GII scores, I used Python to download multiple World Bank API links on R&D expenditure (as % of GDP).





Figure 2 shows that R&D expenditure followed GII scores, with those who saw GII rises showing R&D increases (China, USA, Korea), and those who saw GII falls seeing stagnant R&D (Finland, UK). However, for Belgium and Norway, R&D increases corresponded with declining GII scores, thus the relationship between innovation spending and its output requires further analysis.


Assessing the relationship between Innovative Spend and Output


To account for the lag between R&D expenditure and output, I used Python to construct a three-year average of R&D expenditure for each country. First, I merged this statistic with World Bank data on ‘High-Tech’ exports in percentages and current US$.

Second, I scraped data on scientific publications from ScimagoJR and used World Bank population data to calculate 'Publications-per-million' (PPM), which accounts for population size. Then, I regressed R&D expenditure on each innovative output to create bubble and scatter charts, with both regressions displaying positive correlations between innovative spending and outputs.





Figure 3 shows East-Asia outperforming the West, with China and Singapore having higher ‘High-Tech’ export percentages than the US, despite spending less of GDP on R&D.

Figure 4’s R2 statistic of 0.32 suggests 32% of ‘PPM’ variation was explained by R&D expenditure. Notably, Switzerland’s high ‘PPM’ value and R&D statistic may explain its top GII rankings.


The Ease of Innovation


To examine the ease of innovation across countries, I used the Economist's "Business Environment Rankings" and OECD data on ‘Venture Capital’ (investments on new ‘high-potential’ businesses). After scraping and cleaning the Economist's PDF, I created a bar chart showing BER changes for the Top12 at 2018-end.





Singapore and Switzerland’s Top-2 ranks may explain their strong performances in ‘High-Tech’ exports (Figure 3) and GII (Figure 1), respectively.

Next, I cleaned the OECD data and calculated the 2-year percentage changes in VC to filter out year-on-year fluctuations. A resulting trellis chart was created, displaying VC trends for 12 countries.





Figures 5-6 illustrate that Countries with thriving BERs showed significant increases in VC (Switzerland, USA), while countries with stagnant BERs saw lower VC growth or declines (Sweden, Finland).


Going Forward; Considering the 'Talent'


To assess the talent across countries, I scraped the Webometrics website to create a stacked bar chart showing the number of Top-1000 universities in countries hosting at least one Top-100 university. I also scraped the ‘Global Entrepreneurship Index’ from GEDI, and regressed it on the percentage of individuals holding master's degrees (World Bank).




Figure 7 demonstrates the US’ domination in the quality and quantity of higher education institutions, with China, UK and Germany following. However, Switzerland and Netherlands also perform well in the Top-100 given their land size.

Figure 8 shows a positive correlation between higher-education attainment and entrepreneurship performance, although the R2 of 0.14 suggests a relatively weak relationship. Notably, many countries with high GEI scores and proportions with master's degrees also performed well in the GII (e.g US, Switzerland, Netherlands).



Concluding Statements


This project has demonstrated that successful innovation requires a combination of factors, including investing in R&D, creating an attractive business environment, and cultivating a talented workforce through education development.

These findings can inform future research on innovation and identify improvement areas for countries seeking to emulate the thriving innovation ecosystems of top performers like Singapore and Switzerland.



Data Challenges (see Colab files throughout page)

Scraping data from Webometrics proved challenging, as I was blocked from web-scraping using ‘pandas’ (pd.read_html), so I used 'Beautiful Soup' to inspect the website's elements and scrape the table. Then, I used 'Tabulate' to convert the scraped data into table format and converted it to long-form using the '.melt' command.

Common issues during data cleaning included name mismatches between data sources (Figures 1,3,8) and missing values (‘NaN’) for certain countries, which primarily affected World Bank and OECD data. To address name discrepancies, “match” and “.replace” commands in Excel and Python were used. To deal with missing values for the R&D statistic (Figures 3-4), 2015, 2017, and 2019 were used for the 3-year average to limit the number of missing values and ensure the inclusion of important countries such as Switzerland, who only published R&D data every two years.
